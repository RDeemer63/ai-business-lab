# Retrospective — Experiment Zero

**Date:** 2026-07-09
**Format:** Seven questions, short answers.
**Purpose:** Honest accounting before the studio moves to Experiment 001. Once this is filed, Experiment Zero is closed. Its lessons live here and in POST_MORTEM.md.

---

## 1. What surprised us?

The review cycle caught more problems than expected — and that was a good sign, not a bad one. The original validation plan had six material issues: legal positioning errors, a broken workload math, and research claims that were either unsourced or factually wrong (Winn-Dixie, DOJ rule scope). The review cycle working was the surprise, not the errors themselves. Systems that catch problems early are behaving correctly.

---

## 2. What wasted time?

The three-version revision cycle on the validation plan. v1 had compounding errors that required a full rewrite in v2, then a targeted fix in v3. A lighter internal check before Ryan reviewed v1 might have caught the legal positioning and workload math issues before they required a full Issue and formal response. The cost was not severe — one day total — but the pattern is worth noting.

---

## 3. What almost broke?

The KPI. The v2 plan quietly changed "under 2 hours" to "under 2h20m" in the acceptance criteria. If the Stage Gate review had not caught that shift, the precedent would have been set that KPIs are adjustable when estimates are inconvenient. That precedent would have made every future KPI meaningless. The Stage Gate worked. But it was close.

---

## 4. What should never happen again?

A validation plan should never quietly change an experiment KPI to match an estimate. If the estimate exceeds the KPI, the workflow changes. If the workflow cannot change enough to meet the KPI, the experiment records failure and the studio learns. The bar does not move. This rule is now in STAGE_GATE_LESSONS.md.

---

## 5. What should become a standard for every experiment?

Three things earned their way to permanent standard:

- Agents prepare all personalized outreach drafts. Founders approve and trigger. Founders do not produce.
- Every step in every workflow has an explicit Definition of Done and Stop Condition before work begins.
- Any market research containing legal or statistical claims requires a Sourced Fact Table before it is used in marketing copy or a validation plan.

---

## 6. What should stay in the parking lot?

The batch scanner build. It was needed for execution, but execution never ran. Building it now would be building infrastructure for a business the studio is not pursuing. If a future experiment uses scan-based personalization as the hook, pull the scanner build off the parking lot then. Not before.

The experiment scorecard format is new. One cycle is not enough to know if the categories are right. Run two more experiments before treating the scorecard as fixed.

---

## 7. What evidence changed our minds?

The Chief Architect's correction on "yes" vs "probably — and we now have evidence." The instinct after a successful planning cycle is to call it a pass. The discipline is to say: the planning cycle worked; the execution was not tested; we have evidence for one and not the other. That distinction is evidence-based reasoning applied to the studio's own results. It is the same standard the studio is supposed to apply to market results. Applying it internally is the harder and more important habit.
