# Kill Criteria

Conditions that trigger a no-go decision. When any one of these conditions is met, the business is killed. No negotiation. No "one more test." No extensions.

Killing an idea when these conditions are met is the machine working correctly. A killed idea produces a knowledge base entry. A kept-alive idea that does not meet criteria produces debt.

---

## Hard kill conditions (any one is sufficient)

**1. Zero genuine buying intent after a 7-day validation test**
The test ran. Real people saw the offer. Zero of them expressed genuine interest in paying. Not low interest. Zero. One expression of genuine buying intent restarts the clock for one more iteration.

"Genuine buying intent" means: a prospect said they would pay, asked for pricing, requested a demo, or asked how to sign up. Comments like "interesting idea" and "good luck with that" do not count.

**2. Two consecutive iterations with no improvement in signal**
The first test produced weak signal. The offer was revised. The second test produced equally weak signal or weaker. Two consecutive iterations with no improvement means the idea is not responding to iteration. Kill it.

**3. Ryan's estimated time-to-stabilize exceeds 20 hours**
Based on what the validation test revealed about the delivery requirements, Ryan would need more than 20 hours of personal involvement to get the business to its stable operating state (under 5 hours/week). This makes the business incompatible with the studio model.

**4. Required skill cannot be acquired by the studio**
The delivery of the service requires a capability that the studio's agent infrastructure cannot develop within 30 days. This is not about current capability -- it is about whether the capability is acquirable. If it is not, the idea requires a different organization to execute.

**5. Delivery cannot be documented in a single workflow after 30 days of operation**
The business has been operating for 30 days and the delivery process still cannot be completely described in one workflow file. At this point, the business requires ongoing human judgment at the execution level, not just the oversight level.

## Soft kill conditions (require Ryan's judgment)

These do not automatically trigger a kill but should prompt a serious evaluation:

- The buyer turns out to be harder to reach than estimated and acquisition cost is unsustainable
- Client retention at 30 days is below 50% with no clear explanation or improvement path
- A competitor launches an identical product at a lower price point before the studio reaches $5k MRR
- Ryan's interest in the business has declined to the point where oversight feels like a burden

Soft kill conditions are flagged by agents as Needs Ryan items. Ryan evaluates and decides.

---

## What happens after a kill

1. Librarian records the kill in `09-lessons/post-mortems/` with the conditions met, what was learned, and what the next idea should avoid
2. KNOWLEDGE_BASE.md is updated with the key lesson
3. The idea file in `01-business-ideas/` is updated with a "Status: Killed" header and the date
4. NEXT_ACTIONS.md is updated to move to the next idea

The kill is documented completely before the next idea is started. No undocumented kills.

---

## What is not a kill condition

- The business is harder than expected (hard is normal)
- The first test produced weak signal (one test is not enough to kill)
- Ryan is not excited about it (excitement is not an investment criterion)
- A different idea looks more attractive (pursuing a shinier option is a bias, not a reason)

Comparison to other ideas is only valid after the current idea has completed at least one full validation test. Do not kill an idea because something else looks better. Kill it only when the kill conditions are met.
