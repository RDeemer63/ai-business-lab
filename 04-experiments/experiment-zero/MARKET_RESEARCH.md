## Market Size

### ADA Lawsuit Volume

ADA Title III website accessibility lawsuits have grown significantly since 2018:

| Year | Federal ADA Title III Suits (approx.) | Source |
|------|---------------------------------------|--------|
| 2018 | 2,300 | Seyfarth Shaw Annual ADA Report |
| 2019 | 2,900 | Seyfarth Shaw |

These are federal filings only. New York State court filings (under the NYCHRL) add thousands more annually. State-level filings in California and Florida are also substantial.

### Who Gets Sued

The businesses being targeted are overwhelmingly small-to-mid-sized. Serial plaintiffs and their law firms operate as volume litigators. Settlement is cheaper and faster than defense, so the model works against businesses without dedicated legal departments.

Top industries by lawsuit frequency (Seyfarth Shaw, UsableNet data):
1. Retail and e-commerce (30%+ of all suits)
2. Food service and restaurants (20%+)
3. Healthcare and medical practices (15%)
4. Travel, hospitality, and hotels (10%)
5. Financial services and insurance (8%)
6. Entertainment and media (5%)
7. Other (12%)

### How Many Businesses Are Exposed

The US Census Bureau reports approximately 33 million small businesses in the US. Industry research (Statista, SBA) suggests roughly 70-80% of businesses with 10+ employees have a website — approximately 5-6 million businesses in the target range.

Of those, studies from WebAIM (Web Accessibility In Mind) scanning the top 1 million websites found that 96.3% had detectable WCAG 2.1 failures on their homepage alone. The same failure rate almost certainly applies to small business websites.

Realistic addressable market for cold outreach: businesses with 10-100 employees, in high-lawsuit industries, with a web presence. Conservative estimate: 1.5-2 million US businesses.

## The Buyer

### Who Gets the Email

**Primary buyer:** Business owner or office manager at a company with 10-50 employees.

**Secondary buyer:** Marketing manager or digital manager at a company with 50-200 employees.

**Characteristics of the primary buyer:**
- Revenue: $500K to $5M annually
- Industry: Restaurant, retail, healthcare, professional services
- Website: Has a website, did not build it themselves, probably runs on WordPress or a website builder
- Legal awareness: Has heard of ADA lawsuits but believes "it won't happen to me" until it does
- Decision cycle: Can decide to spend $79-$149/month without a committee
- Pain tolerance: Would rather pay $99/month indefinitely than risk a $30,000+ settlement

**What they fear:**
Not accessibility itself. The lawsuit. Specifically: a demand letter arriving with a 30-day settlement deadline and no good options. The fear is not "my site is inaccessible" — it is "I'm going to get sued and it's going to cost me money I don't have."

**What triggers action:**
A peer getting sued. A news story. A demand letter showing up. Or receiving a scan of their own site showing specific violations with a cost attached to ignoring them.

## Pain Evidence

### Real Lawsuit Data

Robles v. Domino's Pizza (9th Circuit, 2019): Domino's was sued because its website and app were not accessible to blind users using screen readers. The 9th Circuit held that the ADA applies to websites of businesses with physical locations. Domino's appealed to the Supreme Court, which declined to hear the case. Domino's subsequently reached a settlement. This case established that website accessibility is not a grey area in the 9th Circuit.

Winn-Dixie (11th Circuit, 2022): A blind man sued Winn-Dixie because its website was not compatible with screen readers. Court found in favor of the plaintiff. Winn-Dixie was ordered to make its website compliant and pay attorney's fees.

Serial plaintiff operations: Firms like the law office of Yitzchak Zelman, Gottlieb & Associates, and several California-based firms file hundreds of ADA website accessibility suits per year. These are not disability advocacy organizations — they are volume litigants. A single plaintiff may appear in 50-200 suits per year.

### Typical Settlement Costs

Based on publicly available data from law firms that defend ADA suits:
- Average settlement (without litigation): $25,000-$50,000 including attorney's fees
- Settlement with court involvement: $50,000-$150,000
- Contested trial: $200,000+
- DOJ enforcement action: Injunctive relief + potential civil penalties

A business paying $99/month for monitoring ($1,188/year) is spending less than 5% of the cheapest settlement amount to reduce their exposure.

### Documented Demand Letter Process

The standard workflow for serial plaintiffs:
1. Run automated scan of a website (takes minutes)
2. Document violations (screenshot, automated report)
3. Send demand letter claiming to have attempted to use the website and been unable to
4. Offer to settle for $10,000-$30,000 before filing
5. File if settlement declined
6. Repeat across hundreds of businesses

The cost to them per letter is approximately $50-100 in labor. The expected value per letter is positive even with low settlement rates.

### Current Market Urgency

The DOJ published final regulations on web accessibility under Title II of the ADA in April 2024 (for state and local governments). While this applies to government entities, it signals continued regulatory attention and likely extension of formal rulemaking to Title III (private businesses) in the near future.

Multiple plaintiff-side law firms have publicly stated they are expanding ADA website accessibility practices. Law firms on the defense side (Seyfarth Shaw, Fisher Phillips) publish annual reports warning clients that this is a growing risk category.

Search volume for "ADA website compliance" has grown consistently since 2019 (Google Trends data). Terms like "ADA lawsuit small business" and "website ADA requirements" show sustained elevated search volume.
