## Correction Notice

The original version of this document contained three errors. They are corrected below. See the Sourced Fact Table at the end of this document before using any claim in marketing copy.

**Correction A — Winn-Dixie removed:** Gil v. Winn-Dixie Stores was cited as supporting website-accessibility liability. The 11th Circuit Court of Appeals reversed the trial court decision in 2021, holding that Winn-Dixie's website was not a "place of public accommodation" under the ADA in that circuit. The case is not valid supporting precedent. It has been removed and replaced with Robles v. Domino's Pizza.

**Correction B — DOJ April 2024 rule scoped correctly:** The April 2024 DOJ final web accessibility rule applies to Title II entities (state and local governments) only. It does not create WCAG 2.1 AA requirements for private businesses under Title III. The original document implied otherwise. Language has been corrected to reflect actual scope.

ADA Title III lawsuits cover physical and digital accessibility barriers. Website-specific suits are a growing share of the total. The figures below are all ADA Title III federal filings — not website-only — unless otherwise noted.

Website-accessibility-specific federal filings: UsableNet tracked approximately 3,255 federal website/app ADA suits in 2022 and 4,605 in 2023. These are a subset of the total Title III numbers above.

Source for website-specific counts: UsableNet, "2023 ADA Web Accessibility Year in Review," published January 2024.

### Who Gets Sued

Serial plaintiffs and their law firms operate as volume litigators. Settlement is cheaper and faster than defense, so the model works against businesses without dedicated legal departments.

Industries by lawsuit frequency (UsableNet 2023 data, website-specific suits):
1. Retail and e-commerce (largest share)
2. Food service and restaurants
3. Healthcare and medical practices
4. Travel, hospitality, and hotels
5. Financial services

Exact percentages vary by year and data source. Do not cite industry percentages in marketing copy without verifying against the current UsableNet report.

### How Many Businesses Are Exposed

The US Census Bureau reports approximately 33 million small businesses in the US (2022 data). Businesses with 10+ employees and a web presence represent the realistic target segment. An exact count of this subset is not available from a single authoritative source.

WebAIM (Web Accessibility In Mind) scans the top 1 million websites annually. Their 2024 report found that 95.9% of home pages had detectable WCAG 2.1 failures. This is the top 1 million sites — smaller business sites likely have equal or higher failure rates, but this specific stat does not apply to small businesses directly.

Source: WebAIM "The WebAIM Million — 2024 Accessibility Analysis," published March 2024.

## Pain Evidence

### Legal Precedent

**Robles v. Domino's Pizza LLC (9th Circuit, 2019):**
Guillermo Robles, a blind man, sued Domino's because its website and app were not compatible with screen readers. The 9th Circuit Court of Appeals held that the ADA applies to websites of businesses that have physical locations. Domino's petitioned the Supreme Court, which declined to hear the case in 2019. The case proceeded to the district court level. This is the strongest current federal appellate precedent supporting website accessibility requirements for businesses with physical locations in the 9th Circuit (AK, AZ, CA, HI, ID, MT, NV, OR, WA).

Note: This precedent applies within the 9th Circuit. Other circuits have not uniformly agreed. The legal landscape is unsettled outside the 9th Circuit.

Source: Robles v. Domino's Pizza LLC, 913 F.3d 898 (9th Cir. 2019).

**DOJ Guidance (Title III, private businesses):**
The DOJ has repeatedly stated in guidance documents that the ADA applies to websites of businesses that serve the public. However, the DOJ has not issued a formal WCAG standard for private businesses under Title III. The April 2024 DOJ final rule on web accessibility applies to Title II entities only (state and local governments).

Source: DOJ, "Guidance on Web Accessibility and the ADA," March 18, 2022.

### Serial Plaintiff Operations

The standard workflow for serial plaintiffs:
1. Run automated scan of a website (takes minutes)
2. Document violations with a screenshot or automated report
3. Send demand letter claiming to have attempted to use the website and been unable to
4. Offer to settle before filing
5. File suit if settlement declined
6. Repeat

Law firms actively expanding ADA website accessibility practices include plaintiff-side operations identified in Seyfarth Shaw annual reports. This is documented industry behavior, not a fringe activity.

### Settlement Cost Estimates

The following ranges appear in legal defense publications. These are estimates, not guaranteed outcomes:

- Pre-litigation demand settlement: $10,000-$35,000 (commonly cited by defense counsel)
- Settlement after filing: $25,000-$75,000 (attorney's fees included)
- Contested litigation: $100,000+

Sources: Fisher Phillips, "ADA Website Accessibility Lawsuits: What You Need to Know" (2023); Seyfarth Shaw ADA Year in Review reports.

These figures are not guaranteed and depend on jurisdiction, facts, and counsel. Do not present these as definitive in marketing copy.

## Market Signals

### Search Volume

Google Trends shows sustained elevated search volume since 2019 for terms including "ADA website compliance," "ADA website requirements," and "ADA lawsuit small business." Volume increased measurably after high-profile cases and DOJ guidance releases.

Source: Google Trends (publicly available — verify current before citing in marketing).

### Forum Activity

r/smallbusiness, r/legaladvice, and r/entrepreneur have recurring threads from business owners who received ADA demand letters. Common questions: "Is this a scam?" and "What do I do?" Responses consistently point to the legitimacy of the claims and the cost of ignoring them.

This is qualitative market signal, not quantitative data.

## Summary

Website accessibility lawsuits are real, documented, and growing in volume. The buyer is identifiable and reachable. The fear trigger is legitimate. The legal landscape is settled enough in some circuits (9th Circuit) to justify business concern. It is unsettled enough in others that marketing copy must not overstate the legal risk.

The opportunity is to offer a plain-language accessibility monitoring service that helps businesses understand what is broken on their site. Not to promise legal protection.
