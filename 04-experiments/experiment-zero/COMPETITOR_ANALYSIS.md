## Overview

The ADA compliance tool market divides into two segments:

1. **Overlay companies** — bolt a JavaScript widget onto a site, claim it makes the site compliant. Fast to install, legally dubious, widely criticized by accessibility advocates. This is the dominant market segment by revenue.

2. **Audit and monitoring companies** — scan sites, report violations, help businesses understand what needs to be fixed. Smaller segment, more credible, more expensive or more developer-focused.

Our positioning opportunity is in the second segment, at a price point the first segment's customers can afford.

---

## Direct Competitors

### accessiBe

**What they sell:** AI-powered overlay widget. Installs a single line of JavaScript. Claims to automatically remediate accessibility violations in real time.

**Pricing:** $490/year per site (approximately $41/month). Larger sites and plans go higher.

**Market position:** The dominant brand in the small business overlay space. Heavy advertising, aggressive affiliate marketing, strong awareness.

**Critical problems:**
- Over 700 accessibility professionals and organizations signed the "Overlay Fact Sheet" (overlayFactSheet.com) stating overlays do not make sites fully accessible
- Multiple lawsuits have been filed against businesses USING accessiBe, with plaintiffs arguing the overlay did not resolve the underlying violations
- National Federation of the Blind and American Council of the Blind have both issued statements opposing overlay approaches
- G2 reviews (as of research date): 4.2/5 stars, but significant minority of reviewers report continued lawsuits after implementation and frustration with customer service responses to those lawsuits

**Key complaint pattern on G2/Capterra:** "We got sued after using accessiBe. Their support told us to handle it ourselves."

**Our differentiation against them:** We do not use overlays. We monitor and report. We tell you what is broken so you can actually fix it. A client who implements our fixes cannot be told the fix did not work — the fix is in the code, not a widget that can be challenged.

---

### AudioEye

**What they sell:** Overlay widget plus an "accessibility office" that includes some manual remediation. Positioned as more comprehensive than accessiBe.

**Pricing:** Self-service: $49/month. Managed plans (with human remediation): $199-499/month. Enterprise: custom.

**Market position:** Publicly traded (NASDAQ: AEYE). More credible than accessiBe due to human oversight layer, but still overlay-based at lower tiers.

**Critical problems:**
- The $49/month tier is still overlay-only, with the same criticisms as accessiBe
- The managed plans ($199+) are competitive but require talking to sales
- Shared legal exposure to the same overlay backlash

**G2 reviews:** 4.3/5. Similar complaint pattern to accessiBe at the self-service tier.

---

### UserWay

**What they sell:** Overlay widget. Has a free tier (limited). Paid starts around $49/month.

**Market position:** Positioned as the budget/free overlay option. Large install base due to free tier.

**Critical problems:** Same overlay criticisms. The free tier is used by many small businesses who do not understand they are not actually compliant.

---

### Siteimprove

**What they sell:** Full digital experience platform including accessibility auditing, analytics, SEO, and content quality. Enterprise-focused.

**Pricing:** $250-500+/month for small implementations. Custom for enterprise.

**Market position:** The credible enterprise option. Not competing for small business customers directly.

**Why they're not our competition:** Out of budget for small businesses. Not marketed to them. Their sales process involves demos and contracts.

---

### Deque Systems (axe Monitor)

**What they sell:** axe is the industry-standard open-source accessibility testing engine. axe Monitor is their paid monitoring product. axe DevTools is their developer testing tool.

**Pricing:** axe Monitor: approximately $279/month per site (enterprise positioning). axe DevTools: free to $300/month.

**Market position:** Trusted by developers and enterprise teams. Not small-business-friendly in positioning or pricing.

**Why they're not our main competition:** Developer-focused, enterprise-priced. Small business owners do not know what axe is.

---

### Pope Tech

**What they sell:** Website accessibility monitoring and reporting. WCAG-based scanning, no overlay. Reports show specific violations by page, organized by severity.

**Pricing:** $20-150/month depending on page count and reporting needs.

**Market position:** The honest alternative in the mid-market. Well-reviewed by accessibility professionals.

**Critical gap:** Not marketed to small businesses facing legal risk. Marketed to web teams and agencies. Their messaging assumes technical sophistication. A restaurant owner receiving a Pope Tech report would not know what to do with it.

**Takeaway:** Pope Tech proves the monitoring-only model works. Their gap is that they don't translate reports into "here's your legal risk and here's how to fix it."

---

### Bureau of Internet Accessibility (BoIA)

**What they sell:** Accessibility audits (one-time and ongoing), monitoring, and legal risk reports. Positioned specifically around legal risk.

**Pricing:** Audit starting around $3,000-10,000 (one-time). Ongoing monitoring: custom quote, typically $300-1,000/month.

**Market position:** More credible legal-risk-oriented option but priced for mid-market and above.

**Critical gap:** Price point excludes small businesses. No self-serve entry point.

## Positioning Gaps

1. **The overlay credibility gap.** The dominant solutions in the affordable range (accessiBe, AudioEye, UserWay) are under serious credibility attack. Accessibility professionals uniformly oppose them. Lawsuits are being filed against businesses using them. A business that pays for an overlay and then gets sued is angry and looking for an alternative.

2. **The plain-language gap.** Every monitoring tool (Pope Tech, Deque, Siteimprove) produces technical reports that assume the reader understands WCAG criteria. A restaurant owner does not know what "ARIA landmark" means. Nobody translates violation data into "here is your lawsuit risk and here are the three things to fix first."

3. **The small-business price point with monitoring (not overlay).** Under $150/month for honest monitoring with plain-language reports does not exist at meaningful quality in the market.

4. **The "we scanned your site" cold outreach hook.** No competitor is leading acquisition with a free scan + specific results sent cold. They use paid ads and content marketing. A personalized "we found 47 violations on your site, here's the report" email is a hook nobody in this space is using at scale.

## Our Differentiation Angle

**Position:** The only ADA compliance monitoring service that tells small businesses exactly what is broken and what to fix — without an overlay, without technical jargon, and without a five-figure consulting bill.

**Why this is defensible:**
- Overlay backlash is growing, not shrinking. Being "not an overlay" is increasingly a feature, not a detail.
- Plain-language is a product choice, not a technology choice. It cannot be instantly copied — it requires rewriting all reporting UX.
- The cold-scan acquisition model is a channel advantage. It is not patentable, but it requires operational infrastructure that takes time to build.

**The honest competitive acknowledgment:** If accessiBe or AudioEye decides to build a monitoring-only product with plain-language reports, they can. They have the distribution. The window for establishing a position is 12-24 months, not permanent. This is an early-mover opportunity, not a moat.

## Customer Complaints About Competitors (Common Themes)

From G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, and Reddit:

1. "Got sued after installing their widget. They said it wasn't their problem."
2. "The widget breaks my website's existing functionality."
3. "The report showed we were 100% compliant and then we got a demand letter two months later."
4. "We couldn't tell what actually needed to be fixed — just a long list of error codes."
5. "Their customer service disappears as soon as you have a problem."
6. "We don't know if the fixes actually worked. No ongoing monitoring."

These complaints are direct product requirements for our positioning.
